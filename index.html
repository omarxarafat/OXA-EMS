Module 1

Introduction to ISO 14001 & the EMS

What is an Environmental Management System?

An Environmental Management System (EMS) is a structured framework that helps our factory manage its environmental responsibilities systematically. It is not just about compliance — it is about integrating environmental thinking into everything we do, from purchasing raw materials to shipping finished cables.

The PDCA Cycle — Our Engine for Improvement

ISO 14001 is built on the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) cycle. This is the heartbeat of our EMS:

Plan

Identify environmental aspects, set objectives and targets, plan actions.

Do

Implement operational controls, train employees, follow procedures.

Check

Monitor performance, conduct audits, review compliance.

Act

Take corrective actions, improve the system, celebrate successes.

Key point: The PDCA cycle never stops — it is a continuous loop of improvement.

Benefits of ISO 14001 in Our Factory

  • Reduced waste and raw material costs — less copper scrap, less PVC waste.
  • Energy savings through optimized production processes.
  • Compliance with environmental laws — avoiding fines and penalties.
  • Improved reputation with customers, regulators, and the community.
  • Safer workplace — fewer spills, accidents, and hazardous exposures.

Environmental Aspects & Impacts

Understanding Aspects and Impacts

An environmental aspect is any element of our activities, products, or services that can interact with the environment. The environmental impact is the change that occurs as a result.

Simple example: Operating an extrusion machine (activity) → consumes electricity (aspect) → depletes natural resources and contributes to climate change (impact).

Three Conditions We Consider

Normal

Routine operations — daily production, regular cleaning, scheduled maintenance.

Abnormal

Planned but non-routine — machine startups, shutdowns, maintenance shutdowns.

Emergency

Unplanned events — chemical spills, fires, equipment failures, leaks.

Module 6

Operational Control — Procedures & Work Instructions

Operational controls are the specific procedures, work instructions, and practices that ensure our activities are carried out in a way that minimizes environmental impact. Every critical operation has a documented control.

Chemical Receiving & Storage

  1. Verify the chemical matches the purchase order.
  2. Check container integrity — no leaks, no damage.
  3. Ensure SDS is received and filed before storage.
  4. Store in designated area with secondary containment.
  5. Update the chemical register within 24 hours.

Production Line Start-up

  1. Check all emission control equipment is operational.
  2. Verify raw materials are correct and within spec.
  3. Ensure waste collection bins are in place and empty.
  4. Document pre-start inspection in the logbook.
  5. Report any abnormalities immediately.
NEVER bypass an operational control. If a control is not working, stop the job and report it.
Module 7

Waste & Scrap Management

Waste Classification in Our Factory

We classify waste into three main categories to ensure proper handling and disposal:

TypeExamplesDisposal
Non-HazardousCopper scrap, PVC trimmings, paper, wood palletsRecycling or licensed waste contractor
HazardousUsed oil, chemical rags, lubricant containers, spent filtersLicensed hazardous waste contractor only
GeneralOffice waste, food packaging, non-contaminated materialsMunicipal waste collection

Warehouses

  • Segregate chemicals by compatibility — acids separate from bases, oxidizers separate from flammables.
  • All liquid chemicals must have secondary containment (drip trays, bunded pallets).
  • Inspect storage areas weekly for leaks, corrosion, or damaged containers.
  • Keep minimum 1 meter clearance between chemical shelves and walls.
  • Flammable storage must be in fire-rated cabinets with ventilation.
  • Never store incompatible chemicals together — refer to the segregation chart.

Scrap Handling

  • Scrap copper is a valuable resource — keep it clean and separate from other waste.
  • PVC scrap goes into dedicated clear bags for recycling.
  • NEVER put hazardous waste (oily rags, chemical containers) into scrap bins.
  • All scrap must be weighed and recorded before leaving the factory.
  • Report any scrap bin contamination immediately to your supervisor.
  • Use separate bins: blue = copper, green = PVC, yellow = general waste.

Module 8

Warehouses & Chemical Management

Chemical Storage Best Practices

  • Store all chemicals in designated areas only.
  • Maintain a current chemical inventory with quantities.
  • All containers must be labeled with the full chemical name and hazard pictograms.
  • Keep SDS binder in an accessible location — know where it is.
  • Incompatible chemicals must be stored separately (e.g., acids vs. chlorine compounds).
  • Spill kits must be located within 10 meters of chemical storage areas.
  • Weekly inspections are mandatory — use the chemical inspection checklist.

How to Read a Safety Data Sheet (SDS)

An SDS has 16 sections. As an operator, the most important sections to know are:

SectionWhat It Tells You
Section 2 — HazardsHazard classification, pictograms, signal words, precautionary statements.
Section 4 — First AidWhat to do in case of exposure — inhalation, skin contact, eye contact, ingestion.
Section 5 — FirefightingSuitable extinguishing media, specific hazards, protective equipment.
Section 6 — Accidental ReleaseSpill procedures, containment methods, clean-up instructions.
Section 7 — Handling & StorageSafe handling practices, storage conditions, incompatibilities.

Module 9

Emergency Preparedness & Response

General Emergency Principles — R.A.C.E.

Remove

Remove people from danger

Alert

Alert others & call for help

Contain

Contain the hazard if safe

Evacuate

Evacuate if necessary

Chemical Spill
  1. R — Remove anyone from the immediate area.
  2. A — Alert colleagues and supervisor. Call the emergency number.
  3. C — If trained and safe: use the spill kit to absorb and contain.
  4. E — Evacuate if the spill is large or toxic fumes are present.
Spill kits are located at: warehouse entrance, extrusion area, chemical storage room.
Fire
  1. R — Remove people from the immediate danger zone.
  2. A — Activate the nearest fire alarm. Alert the fire warden.
  3. C — Use the correct extinguisher (CO₂ or dry powder for electrical fires).
  4. E — Evacuate to the assembly point. Do NOT use elevators.
Know your nearest: fire alarm point, fire extinguisher, emergency exit, assembly area.

Other Emergency Scenarios

Medical Emergency

  • Call the first aider / medical room immediately.
  • Do NOT move an injured person unless they are in immediate danger.
  • Keep the area clear and provide first aid if trained.

Gas Leak / Fume Release

  • Evacuate immediately. Do NOT attempt to locate the source.
  • Alert the emergency response team from a safe location.
  • If possible, shut down ventilation to prevent spread.

Module 12

Nonconformity & Corrective Action

What is a Nonconformity?

A nonconformity is a failure to meet a requirement. This could be:

  • A procedure not being followed (e.g., skipping chemical inspection).
  • A piece of equipment not functioning correctly (e.g., leaky valve).
  • A record not being completed (e.g., missing waste log entry).
  • A legal requirement that has not been met.

Corrective Action Process

  1. Identify and document the nonconformity.
  2. Contain the problem (immediate fix to stop the issue).
  3. Determine the root cause (why did it really happen?).
  4. Implement corrective action to prevent recurrence.
  5. Verify the action was effective.
Do not hide nonconformities — identifying them is the first step to improvement. A hidden problem never gets fixed.

Your Roles & Responsibilities

Follow Procedures

Adhere to all EMS work instructions and standard operating procedures. If you are unsure, ask before acting.

Report Incidents

Report any environmental incident, near-miss, or non-conformity to your supervisor. Early reporting prevents escalation.

Conserve Resources

Turn off equipment when not in use, reduce water waste, segregate scrap correctly, and suggest improvements.

  • You have the right to access environmental training and information.
  • You have the right to suggest improvements without fear of blame.
  • You have the right to refuse to perform a task that violates environmental laws.
Module 14

Continual Improvement & Your Voice

Kaizen — Small Changes, Big Impact

Continual improvement is not just about big projects. Small, everyday improvements by every employee add up to significant environmental and cost benefits. This is the Kaizen approach — always asking: 'How can we do this better?'

How You Can Contribute

  • Suggest improvements to your supervisor or via the suggestion box.
  • Participate in EMS team meetings and toolbox talks.
  • Report inefficiencies — water leaks, compressed air leaks, unnecessary lighting.
  • Share best practices with colleagues.

Examples of Employee-Led Improvements

  • An operator suggested changing the die cleaning schedule — reduced lubricant use by 20%.
  • A warehouse worker reorganized chemical storage — reduced spill risk and improved access.
  • A maintenance technician installed auto-shutoff valves on cooling lines — saved 5,000 liters of water per month.
Your ideas matter. The best improvements often come from the people doing the job every day. Speak up!
Scenarios

Real-Life Scenarios — What Would You Do?

Read each scenario carefully. Think about your answer, then click to see the recommended response.

1 A drum of hydraulic oil in the warehouse has no secondary containment. The floor shows signs of staining. +

Recommended Response:
1. Report immediately to the warehouse supervisor. 2. Place a spill tray or bunded pallet under the drum to contain any leaks. 3. The stained floor area should be cleaned and the waste disposed of as hazardous waste. 4. The incident should be logged in the EMS records as a near-miss. 5. All chemical drums in the warehouse should be checked to ensure they have proper containment.

2 You notice a colleague putting used chemical rags into the general waste bin instead of the hazardous waste container. +

Recommended Response:
1. Politely remind your colleague that chemical-contaminated rags are hazardous waste and must go in the red bin. 2. Explain why — these rags can contaminate the entire waste stream and cause environmental harm. 3. Remove the rags from the general bin and place them in the correct hazardous waste container (wear gloves). 4. Report the incident to your supervisor so a toolbox talk can be arranged on waste segregation.

3 You are working on the extrusion line and notice the fume extraction system is not working properly. The production manager tells you to keep running because there is a deadline. +

Recommended Response:
1. You have the right and responsibility to stop work if there is a risk. 2. Explain that operating without fume extraction violates our environmental policy and may exceed legal emission limits. 3. Offer alternatives — can production be shifted to another line, or can the extraction be repaired quickly? 4. If pressure continues, escalate to the EHS manager or top management. 5. Document the situation — the production manager's instruction and your concerns.

4 You discover a small leak in a coolant pipe near the wire drawing area. Coolant is dripping onto the floor and flowing toward a floor drain. +

Recommended Response:
1. Immediately contain the leak — place a drip tray or absorbent material under the leak. 2. Block the floor drain using a drain cover or absorbent socks to prevent coolant from entering the drainage system. 3. Report to the supervisor and maintenance team for pipe repair. 4. Clean up the spilled coolant and dispose of absorbent materials as hazardous waste. 5. Document the incident and the root cause — is this a recurring issue?

5 An internal auditor asks you: 'How do you know what to do with waste from your machine?' and 'Where is the SDS for the chemical you are using?' +

Recommended Response:
1. Answer confidently: 'I follow the waste segregation procedure posted near my workstation. Copper scrap goes in the blue bin, PVC in the green bin, and hazardous waste in the red bin.' 2. 'The SDS is kept in the binder at the supervisor's office and also available digitally on the EMS portal.' 3. If you do not know an answer, say: 'I am not sure, but I can find out and get back to you.' 4. Remember — auditors are there to help us improve, not to catch us out. Be honest and cooperative.

6 You find an unlabeled container of liquid in the chemical storage area. No one knows what it is. +

Recommended Response:
1. Do NOT open, smell, or taste the container to identify it — this is extremely dangerous. 2. Isolate the container in a ventilated, contained area away from other chemicals. 3. Label it as 'UNKNOWN — DO NOT USE' with the date found. 4. Report to the supervisor and EHS team. 5. Review delivery records and purchase orders to trace the source. 6. If it cannot be identified, arrange for disposal by a licensed hazardous waste contractor.

7 The fire alarm sounds during your shift. You are in the warehouse area. +

Recommended Response:
1. Stop what you are doing immediately. 2. Close any chemical containers or valves if safe to do so (seconds only). 3. Proceed calmly to the nearest emergency exit — do NOT run. 4. Walk to the designated assembly point. 5. Do NOT use elevators. 6. Wait for the all-clear signal from the emergency response team. 7. Do NOT re-enter the building until authorized. 8. Report to your supervisor for headcount confirmation.

8 You have an idea to reduce packaging waste — your cables could be wound on reusable reels instead of single-use wooden spools. +

Recommended Response:
1. Write down your idea clearly — including the environmental benefit, estimated cost savings, and implementation steps. 2. Submit it to your supervisor or through the EMS suggestion system. 3. If possible, find examples from other cable factories that use reusable reels. 4. Offer to be part of a trial or pilot project. 5. Even if the idea is not implemented immediately, it demonstrates initiative and will be recorded as a continual improvement suggestion.
